# The Bridge Story

This is the plain-English product story for PaperTrail.

Imagine a state announces a Rs 50 crore rural bridge under a road or rural works scheme. The press release says development is coming. The question PaperTrail asks is not "who stole the money?" The first question is simpler and more powerful:

```text
What records should exist if this Rs 50 crore bridge is real, fairly priced, properly built, and fully paid?
```

## The Money Story

At the top, Parliament approves a scheme budget and the state approves its matching share where the scheme requires one. No contractor has received money yet. This is permission to spend.

Then the state department prepares a Detailed Project Report, or DPR. The DPR says what the bridge should cost: steel, concrete, labour, earthwork, approach road, design, contingencies. This is the first place PaperTrail pays attention.

If the real bridge should cost Rs 38 crore but the DPR says Rs 50 crore, the extra Rs 12 crore has already been made respectable. Nothing needs to be stolen during the bank transfer. The overpayment is now inside the estimate, the tender, the bill, and the file.

Next comes the tender. In the honest version, several qualified contractors compete and the lowest qualified bidder wins. In the rigged version, a cartel quietly decides who wins, others bid high on purpose, and the winning bid lands close to the inflated estimate.

Then the money moves. It usually does not move as Rs 50 crore in one shot. The contractor is paid through running account bills:

- Mobilization advance to start work.
- Payment after foundation work.
- Payment after pillars.
- Payment after deck and approach work.
- Final payment after completion and defect checks.

Each bill depends on paperwork. A Junior Engineer records quantities in the Measurement Book. An Executive Engineer clears or approves the bill. Treasury or PFMS rails process the payment. The contractor receives money in a bank account.

The simple account flow is:

```text
Consolidated Fund / state share
  -> scheme account
  -> payment instruction
  -> contractor bank account
  -> bridge on the ground
```

## The Leakage Story

For a bridge, the risky part is not usually the final bank transfer. The risky part is the paperwork around it.

| Stage | What can go wrong | What PaperTrail checks |
| --- | --- | --- |
| DPR | Estimate is inflated before money moves | Cost per metre, bridge type, district peers, similar projects |
| Tender | Contractor cartel or tailored tender | Single bidder, bid clustering, repeated winners, estimate-close awards |
| Mobilization advance | Money leaves before visible progress | Advance paid but no work-start evidence |
| Measurement Book | Quantities are overstated | Paid quantity vs photos, geo-tags, inspection, similar material norms |
| Running account bill | Stage is certified too early | Bill date vs milestone evidence |
| Final delivery | Bridge is weaker than paid-for | Quality reports, citizen photos, satellite imagery, monsoon damage, collapse records |

## The Product Story

PaperTrail connects three truths:

```text
Paper truth: DPR, tender, Measurement Book, bill, audit report
Money truth: sanction, release, PFMS/treasury payment, contractor account
Ground truth: geo-tag, satellite image, inspection, citizen photo, bridge condition
```

When these truths agree, PaperTrail can say the record looks consistent.

When they disagree, PaperTrail opens a Sense Signal:

```text
Paper says X.
Money moved Y.
Ground reality shows Z.
This needs review by the right role.
```

That is the whole product in one line.

## Why This Starts With A Bridge

Direct Benefit Transfer can improve the last-mile path for pensions, wages, scholarships, and cash benefits. But a bridge is the contractor side of the fork. DBT does not decide whether the estimate was inflated, the tender was competitive, the concrete was actually poured, or the bridge will survive the first heavy monsoon.

That is why PaperTrail starts here: works and procurement are where public money becomes physical reality.
